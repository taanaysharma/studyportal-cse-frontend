Algorithm Design & Analysis
CSE 221 · Core · Prerequisite: Data Structures
Study Plan
Fundamentals & Complexity▼
  • Start with asymptotic notations — Big-O, Omega, and Theta — and get very comfortable evaluating time and space complexity for any given code
  • Master recurrence relations and solve them using substitution, iteration, and the Master Theorem; this appears in almost every exam
  • Understand Disjoint Set (Union-Find) structures — they appear again in graph algorithms later in the course
Design Techniques▼
  • Divide & Conquer: practice Merge Sort, Quick Sort, and Binary Search deeply — trace through examples and derive their complexities yourself
  • Greedy: understand when greedy works and why. Focus on Activity Selection, Huffman Coding, and Kruskal's/Prim's algorithms
  • Dynamic Programming: start with Fibonacci, Matrix Chain Multiplication, LCS, and 0/1 Knapsack — always identify the substructure and overlapping subproblems
Graph Algorithms & NP▼
  • Study BFS, DFS, Dijkstra, Bellman-Ford, and Floyd-Warshall — practice tracing on given graphs step by step
  • For Backtracking and Branch & Bound (N-Queens, TSP) focus on state-space trees — drawing them helps enormously in exams
  • For NP topics, understand P vs NP conceptually, know examples of NP-hard and NP-complete problems, and be able to describe reductions at a high level
Exam Strategy▼
  • Solve previous year question papers — many questions repeat in structure even if not verbatim
  • Practice writing algorithms neatly in pseudocode; examiners value clarity and correct step sequencing
  • For numerical problems (complexity, recurrence), show all intermediate steps — partial marks are given generously
Resources
Recommended Books▼
Primary Textbook: Introduction to Algorithms (CLRS) — Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, Stein
Textbook: Fundamentals of Computer Algorithms — Horowitz & Sahni
Reference: Algorithms — Dasgupta, Papadimitriou, Vazirani (great for building intuition)
Online Resources▼
NPTEL: Design and Analysis of Algorithms
Stanford: CS161 Schedule & Lecture Notes
Coursera: Algorithms Specialization
Study Strategy
ADA rewards understanding over memorisation. If you can explain why an algorithm works and trace it on a new example, you are prepared. Mugging up code without understanding will fail you in this subject.
Scoring Strategy
Mid-sem is usually more straightforward (complexity, sorting). End-sem gets harder with DP, Backtracking, and NP. Don't leave Dynamic Programming — it consistently carries the highest weightage.
Computer System Organization
CSE 222 / CSE 24222 · Core · Prerequisite: Digital Circuit Design (CSE-214)
Mid-Semester Focus
Basic Organization & Instruction Set▼
  • Start with data formats — fixed-point and floating-point representation; IEEE 754 is commonly tested
  • Understand the basic building blocks of a CPU: registers, ALU, control unit, and how they interact over the bus
  • Study Register Transfer Language (RTL) — be able to write RTL statements for given operations
  • Learn instruction types, addressing modes, and expanding op-codes — these are high-frequency exam topics
Control Unit & Microprogramming▼
  • Understand hardwired vs. microprogrammed control — know the trade-offs and be able to explain both with diagrams
  • Study micro-instruction formats, control memory, and the micro-program sequencer carefully
  • Horizontal vs. vertical micro-programming is a standard exam question — know the differences clearly
End-Semester Focus
Arithmetic & Pipeline▼
  • Practice Booth's algorithm for signed multiplication step by step — this is almost always in the exam
  • Learn the division algorithm and floating-point arithmetic operations
  • Understand AMDAHL's Law — be able to calculate speedup for given scenarios numerically
  • Study pipeline stages, data hazards, control hazards, and structural hazards — and how each is resolved
Memory & I/O Organization▼
  • For memory: understand cache mapping schemes (direct, associative, set-associative) with numerical examples
  • Know how DMA works and its advantages over interrupt-driven and program-controlled I/O
  • Study memory hierarchy — registers → cache → RAM → secondary; understand access time trade-offs
Resources & Notes
Recommended Books▼
Primary Textbook: Computer Architecture — Morris Mano (stick to this for 80% of your preparation)
Reference: Computer Organization & Architecture — William Stallings
Quick Reference: Computer Architecture — Schaum's Outlines (great for solved problems)
Online Resources▼
NPTEL: Computer Organization and Architecture
Study Strategy
This subject is heavily diagram-based. Practice drawing block diagrams of the CPU, control unit, cache memory, and pipeline stages from memory. Examiners reward neat, labeled diagrams even when explanations are incomplete.
Scoring Strategy
Booth's algorithm, cache mapping numericals, and pipeline hazard questions are almost certain to appear. Nail these three and you've secured a solid base score before looking at anything else.
Software Engineering
CSE 24221 · Core · Session: Jan–Jun 2026 · Coordinator: Dr. Vikram Garg
Study Plan
SDLC & Process Models▼
  • Understand all SDLC phases and be able to describe each clearly — this is the foundation of the subject
  • Study all process models: Waterfall, Prototyping, RAD, Incremental, Spiral, Win-Win Spiral, and Agile — know their advantages, disadvantages, and when each is used
  • Agile and Extreme Programming are newer additions — expect at least one question on them in end-sem
Project Management & Estimation▼
  • Study the COCOMO model in detail — both Basic and Intermediate; be able to do numerical problems
  • Understand Function Point analysis and the Putnam model as alternative estimation techniques
  • Learn PERT and CPM for scheduling — draw network diagrams and calculate critical paths
  • Halstead Software Science metrics appear as short theory questions — understand the core measures
Design, Testing & Quality▼
  • Learn how to draw DFD (Data Flow Diagrams) — practise creating them from scratch for given scenarios
  • SRS writing is a practical skill — understand the IEEE template and what goes in each section
  • For testing: know black-box vs. white-box testing, and study Verification & Validation methods
  • Understand ISO standards, CMM levels, and Six Sigma at a conceptual level for theory questions
Resources & Strategy
Recommended Books▼
Primary Textbook: Software Engineering: A Practitioner's Approach — Roger S. Pressman
Textbook: Fundamentals of Software Engineering — Rajib Mall (concise and exam-focused)
Reference: An Integrated Approach to Software Engineering — Pankaj Jalote
Emerging Topics (End-Sem)▼
  • The lecture plan includes newer topics: SOA, Cloud Computing, Low-Code/No-Code, and AI/ML adoption in SE
  • These are likely to appear as short theory questions — read class notes once and grasp the key points
  • Don't ignore these just because they sound unfamiliar — they're easier to answer than algorithmic topics
High Scoring Subject
Software Engineering is one of the most scoring subjects this semester — no heavy mathematics, just clear conceptual theory. If you prepare all topics thoroughly, a 9+ grade is very achievable.
Don't Skip These
Do not skip COCOMO numericals and DFD diagram practice. These are the only calculation-heavy parts of the subject but are very predictable and easy marks if practised even once or twice.
Data Communications
CSE 224 · Core · Coordinators: Dr. Ahirwal, Dr. Surendra Singh, Dr. Vaibhav Soni
Mid-Semester Focus
Signals, Channels & Transmission▼
  • Start with the basics of analog vs. digital signals and data — don't rush this foundation
  • Understand Shannon's theorem and Nyquist's theorem thoroughly; numerical problems on channel capacity appear regularly
  • Know the three types of transmission impairments (attenuation, distortion, noise) and how each affects signal quality
  • Study guided vs. unguided transmission media — their characteristics, bandwidth, and typical use cases
Line Coding & Modulation▼
  • Learn line coding schemes (NRZ, RZ, Manchester, AMI) and be able to draw waveforms for a given bit sequence
  • Understand modulation techniques: ASK, FSK, PSK, and QAM — know the differences and when each is preferred
  • Study multiplexing methods: FDM, TDM, and WDM — with their applications and comparison
End-Semester Focus
Error Detection & Satellite▼
  • Study error detection methods: parity, CRC, and checksum — practice CRC calculations step by step
  • Understand the difference between synchronous and asynchronous transmission clearly
  • Satellite communication concepts (GEO, MEO, LEO) are likely to be theory questions — read them once carefully
Multimedia Networks & Security▼
  • Multimedia networks and compression (MP3, JPEG) are conceptual — understand what they do, not how they work mathematically
  • Cryptography basics (symmetric vs. asymmetric, RSA overview) typically appear as short theory questions at the end of the paper
  • Don't skip streaming and multimedia services — they are easy marks for those who read them
Resources & Notes
Recommended Books▼
Primary Textbook: Data Communications and Networking — B.A. Forouzan (most exam questions are sourced from here)
Reference: Data & Computer Communications — William Stallings
Reference: Digital Communications — Simon Haykin
Online Resources▼
NPTEL: Data Communication Course
Cisco: Networking Resource Centre
Study Strategy
Data Comms has a wide syllabus — 40 lectures across very different topics. Don't try to read everything equally. Prioritise: channel capacity numericals, line coding waveforms, CRC calculations, and modulation comparisons. These four areas cover the bulk of exam marks.
Scoring Strategy
The paper tends to mix easy conceptual questions with a few tougher numericals. Always attempt all parts — even a partially correct CRC calculation or a rough waveform sketch earns partial marks.
Fundamentals of Entrepreneurship
HUM 251 · Core · Dept. of Humanities & Social Sciences · MANIT Bhopal
Study Plan
Core Concepts▼
  • Understand the basics of entrepreneurship — its meaning, nature, scope, types, and factors that influence it (social, psychological, economic, environmental)
  • Learn the characteristics of an entrepreneur and the new-generation categories (social, women, tourism) with examples
  • Rural entrepreneurship is a dedicated unit — understand rural industrialization, its problems, and its importance for backward areas
Industrial Laws▼
  • The Industrial Laws unit is fact-heavy — focus on the name, year, and key provisions of each Act (Factory Act 1948, Minimum Wages Act 1948, Provident Fund Act 1952, etc.)
  • Make a compact table of all Acts with their years and one-line purpose — the fastest revision method before exams
  • SEZ, ISDBI, ISO standards, and New Pension Act 2004 frequently appear as short-answer questions
Motivation Theories & Government Schemes▼
  • Study Maslow's Hierarchy and Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory in detail — standard exam questions every year
  • Know McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y, and the management thinkers (Taylor, Fayol, Mayo, Weber) briefly
  • Government initiatives: memorise the full names and one-line purpose of ATAL Innovation Mission, SAMRIDH, Start-up India Seed Fund, Stand-up India, and MGS
Strategy & Resources
Exam Strategy▼
  • This is a fully theory-based subject — the key skill is writing well-structured, point-wise answers
  • Focus on the class slides and whatever is covered in lectures — the exam stays firmly within that scope
  • Use relevant examples (Tata, Infosys, Start-up India) when answering entrepreneurship questions to add depth to your answers
Recommended Books▼
Textbook: Administrative Thinkers — D. Ravindra Prasad et al., Sterling Publishers
Textbook: Labour & Industrial Laws — Padhi, PHI Learning Private Limited
Reference: Entrepreneurship: Theory, Process & Practice — Howard & Donald, Cengage Learning
High Scoring Subject
Entrepreneurship is one of the easiest subjects to score well in this semester — no numericals, no derivations, just clear structured theory. Students who prepare all five units consistently score 8–10 CGPA equivalents here.
Don't Neglect This
Many CSE students ignore HUM subjects until the last moment. Don't. It takes minimal effort relative to other subjects, and a poor score here drags your overall SGPA down unnecessarily.
